About This Tool

In today's digital world, personal data is constantly collected and stored by websites, apps, and third parties. Many people unknowingly expose sensitive information, increasing the risk of identity theft, fraud, or data breaches. This tool helps you become more aware of your data privacy risks and take steps to protect your personal information. This tool helps you understand the risks associated with sharing personal data online. By selecting the types of data you have shared, our system evaluates your exposure level and provides a risk assessment based on vulnerability and data significance.

✔ Quick & Easy → Just select what data you share and get instant results.

✔ Personalized Results → Your risk level and recommendations are tailored to your selections.

✔ Privacy Awareness → Helps you understand what data is at risk and how to protect yourself.

How it Works

This tool evaluates the risks associated with sharing personal data online. By selecting the types of data you have shared, our system calculates your exposure level and provides a risk assessment based on vulnerability and data significance. Follow the recommendations to protect your personal information and reduce your risk.

How It Works

  1. Select the types of personal data you have shared by checking the boxes (e.g., Name, Email, Location, Bank Account).
  2. Click the "Analyze Risk" button to calculate your data risk level.
  3. View your risk assessment to see:
    • Your overall risk level (Low, Medium, High).
    • A detailed explanation of your risk.
    • Recommendations to improve your privacy and security.

How the Calculations Work

Each type of personal data has a risk score, which is calculated using:

Risk Score = Vulnerability × Significance

Vulnerability (1-5): How likely this data is to be exposed (e.g., Name is commonly shared, while a Bank Account is rarely exposed).

Significance (1-5): How sensitive the data is (e.g., Name has low significance, while Biometric Data is highly sensitive).

Your total risk score is the sum of all selected data types:

  • 1 - 50 → Low Risk
  • 51 - 90 → Medium Risk
  • 90+ → High Risk

How Your Final Assessment Is Determined

In addition to your total score, the website analyzes the type of data you shared to give you a more accurate risk explanation:

  • If you share only Identity data (e.g., Name, Email, Phone) → You get a low-risk message because this data is commonly shared.
  • If you share Location data (e.g., Address, IP) → The system warns you about tracking risks.
  • If you share Financial or Biometric data (e.g., Bank Account, Fingerprint) → The system alerts you about serious security risks.

Reference 1: Privacy and Personal Data Protection

Hoven, J. (2019). Information Technology, Privacy, and the Protection of Personal Data.

Full Text: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/254906455_Information_Technology_Privacy_and_the_Protection_of_Personal_Data

Summary: This study explores how information technology and data collection have increased privacy risks worldwide. It discusses laws, digital security measures, and ethical considerations in preventing data misuse. Hoven highlights how surveillance systems, tracking tools, and data exploitation have become prevalent concerns in healthcare, law enforcement, and marketing. He emphasizes the need for stronger legal frameworks, cryptographic protection, and personal awareness to safeguard data from unauthorized access.

Reference 2: The European Data Protection Regulation

Purtova, N. (2015). The Proposed EU Data Protection Regulation: A Missed Opportunity?

Full Text: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2346693

Summary: Purtova critiques the European Union’s 2015 data protection reform, arguing that it failed to reinforce individuals’ rights against large-scale data collection by corporations and governments. The study discusses how the regulation was a missed opportunity to challenge data monopolies and ensure stronger user consent measures. The paper also highlights how businesses continue to manipulate legal loopholes to collect personal data for advertising and tracking.

Reference 3: Privacy Risk Assessment & Scoring

Agarwal, Y. (2016). Developing a Structured Metric to Measure Privacy Risk in Privacy Impact Assessments.

Full Text: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/304995299_Developing_a_Structured_Metric_to_Measure_Privacy_Risk_in_Privacy_Impact_Assessments

Summary: Agarwal introduces a three-step methodology for evaluating privacy risks in IT systems. The study proposes a scoring system that organizations can use to measure privacy risks associated with data collection, storage, and sharing. This method helps businesses and researchers compare risks over time and develop strategies to enhance privacy impact assessments (PIAs).

Reference 4: Emerging Threats in Digital Privacy

Arshad, S. & Asghar, M. (2024). Quantifying Privacy Risks in an Interconnected World.

Full Text: https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/8102366

Summary: Arshad and Asghar explore modern privacy challenges, focusing on the rapid expansion of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and IoT (Internet of Things). The study discusses how user behavior is increasingly tracked through algorithms, surveillance tools, and real-time data processing. It also highlights cybersecurity threats, such as unauthorized recordings, online behavioral profiling, and AI-based privacy breaches.

Reference 5: Data Brokering & Market Trends

Beauvisage, T., et al. (2020). The Growth and Influence of Data Brokers in the Information Industry.

Full Text: https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/12075.003.0005

Summary: This study examines the business models of data brokers, companies that collect, analyze, and sell personal data to third parties. Beauvisage discusses how companies like Acxiom and Equifax have built extensive consumer databases and the ethical concerns surrounding their practices. The paper highlights the economic value of personal data and the risks of individuals unknowingly sharing information that is later used for marketing, surveillance, and profiling.
